Saying a market should exist and saying a market exists do not mean the same thing.

If you say a company exists, that's just a fact. But if you say a company should exist, that's a value judgment. There's an important distinction there that often gets conflated.

## The Efficient Market Hypothesis

If you look at something like the efficient market hypothesis, it was very popular during the Reagan era in the 80s, where academics argued that the market is completely efficient—any information you bring to the market is already factored in.

We can say that now, and maybe it's even mostly true. But I just don't think that the efficient market hypothesis is correct. There just isn't a world where it's 100% correct because quantitative hedge funds exist, because various forms of private arbitrage exist.

There's garbage in the market, there's information missing from the market that would lead to different pricing than what you see. All of these—quantitative hedge funds, private equity, any sort of investor activity—is based on arbitrage. The idea is that the market composition says one thing, but your private position says another, and you believe your position is correct. That difference—that gap—is the money-making opportunity.

## The "King Making" Fallacy

Going back to deciding whether companies or industries should exist: I think it's very contrary to how we should be looking at things.

Consider "king making" in startup land, where people perceive king making as startups that get funded by top firms like Sequoia or Benchmark. If they get funded by these top VCs, people think it's a form of king making where these startups are expected to succeed because these investors said yes.

That's just a signal. But again, it's like trying to subvert the market. People want the market to think certain things. They want the market to think, "Oh, this is the future, the market should move forward." Maybe we'll see a Series B for this startup.

But really? I mean, honestly, who fucking cares? It doesn't matter who invests. It's not really about what should exist. It's about what the market decides.

## The Fallacy of "Should"

Saying an industry should exist is like saying, "Hey, look at this thing in this political climate, in this economic climate, therefore it should exist."

Slavery existed back in the day. By that logic, it should have existed. That's just stupid.

Faxes existed in the 80s, the 70s. Should the fax industry exist? It got overrun by new technology.

I think it's kind of an outdated way of thinking. There are people that think they can predict the market, which I just don't think is true.

## The Communist Russia Example

Look at something like communism in Russia. There's a common joke in the finance community that says if they couldn't look toward Western prices—they would have failed. The idea that a government can just wake up and say, "Okay, bread is now 20 dollars"—with no market backing, no liquidity, no testing of it, no market movement—that's kind of a silly idea.

You can't just price something however you want. That's not true. The nice part about markets is that they actually do price things. They do price things relatively well. Most markets are relatively efficient, but a 100% efficient market is like fucking impossible.

Even 5% is impossible to get because the world just doesn't take that much information that fast.

Obviously there are companies that try to do this now—they try to bake as much information into the market as possible. Maybe there will be some form of quantitative trading where AI can ping everything and find all future opportunities. But I just don't know that that's going to be true.

## The Academic Journal Example

The reason this idea came to my head was because of a debate I was having about the publishing industry for scientific and academic journals.

I was beating back someone who said, "Yes, scientific and academic journals should be a thing"—even though they charge exorbitant fees, even though researchers are often forced to pay into it.

It's not my idea of how things should be. It's not a good model for pushing the frontier with research. It's really just a great way to push greed into research, which I don't think is a good thing.

But saying that the academic journal industry should exist because it does exist is just not an argument. It's just insufficient.

If you look at the academic journal industry, you can see the real issue they were trying to solve. I believe it was the influx of new papers post-WWII, and also a lack of academic scrutiny or testing of new papers. Peer review solved it anyway, but it was a way to try to fix it.

But was that the issue they were trying to solve? I just don't know that it's true with the advent of many of these preprint studies trying to get away from these types of paywalls. I think there's a fundamental mismatch in how these academic journals are working.

## Conclusion

The real question we need to be asking isn't "Does this industry exist?" but rather "Is this industry solving an actual problem in a sustainable way?"

You really need to look at the economics of the market—the economics of what this industry is serving. Just because something exists doesn't mean it should.
