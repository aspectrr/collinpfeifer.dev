I was talking to someone I just met, and they were telling me about their startup idea. But they were explaining it in the most complicated way possible—it was almost like they wanted it to sound more complex than it actually was.

The idea itself could be explained simply. Sure, the simple version doesn't capture every minute detail. But that's the version you tell people. It's the version people understand.

Things are always better when they're simple.

Charlie Munger said that the best entrepreneurs just have one simple idea and they take it incredibly seriously. One simple insight, executed with total seriousness. Microsoft and Apple believed computers would go mainstream and everyone would own one. Facebook believed everyone wants to be connected. These are simple ideas.

Maybe they didn't realize at the time that's what they were building, but it still holds true.

I'm not immune to this. I struggle with making things too complex or trying to explain every detail. I'm not perfect. When I was working at Deer, Fluid, or Steel, I would over-explain these things. When you're in the muck, thinking about an idea 24/7, it's hard to separate the intricate details from that simple core idea you tell people.

Take Steel for example: "browsers for AI agents." That's it. No reason to talk about CDNs, load balancing, distributed clouds. None of that matters in the explanation.

Fluid? "Replicas for agents." Short and sweet. People would ask what replicas are, and I'd say "copies of your environment." They'd get it.

But I used to make this super complicated, and it was really hard to explain.

## Keep It Simple

The other day I was trying to help this person I met. I was trying to bring their idea down to its simplest form, something they could explain to others. After a while, I got it down to something simple. Their response? "Yeah, sure, I guess."

I almost wanted to say: "Dude, it's not good for things to be super complex."

Investors don't care about complexity. Customers don't care. Users don't care.

I think it's something about being an engineer. You don't feel like you're doing real work unless things are complex. But you have to fight that complexity urge. There's just no reason for most things to be super complex.

Most ideas can be explained in less than 25 words. Probably even less than 10.

In this case, I was able to describe it in three words. Yet he spent probably 2,000 words trying to explain it to me, and I'm thinking "what the fuck are you talking about?"

## The 90/10 Rule

This is what I call the 90/10 principle. The simple version captures 90% of what matters. The other 10% is detail, nuance, complexity that you can work through later. But you need that simple 90% to communicate clearly.

If you just have a simple idea to begin with, you avoid this whole problem. Have a simple idea, execute it, and boom. You don't have to make something complex sound simple. You just talk about something that's simple and how simple it is.

The best ideas are simple ones that are taken very seriously.
